What is it?
Metformin is a tablet you take to manage blood sugar levels in Type 2 diabetes. It's usually the first medicine doctors prescribe when you're diagnosed.
How does it help?
Your liver constantly releases sugar into your blood — even when you haven't eaten. Metformin tells your liver to slow this down, so your blood sugar doesn't spike as much throughout the day.
Taking it day to day
You'll typically take it 2 times a day with meals. Taking it with food is important — it reduces stomach discomfort which is the most common complaint when starting.
⚠ Watch out for
Some people feel nauseous or have loose stools when they first start. This usually settles in 1–2 weeks. If you're having a scan with dye (contrast) at a hospital, tell your doctor — you may need to temporarily stop Metformin.
💡 Good to know
Unlike many diabetes medicines, Metformin does NOT cause weight gain or low blood sugar on its own. It's been used safely for over 30 years and is on the WHO list of essential medicines.

Mechanism of Action

Metformin works primarily by inhibiting hepatic glucose production (gluconeogenesis) in the liver. It activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), which improves insulin sensitivity and reduces intestinal glucose absorption. Unlike sulfonylureas, it does not stimulate insulin secretion and is not associated with hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy.
